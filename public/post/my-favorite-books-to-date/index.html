My Favorite Books to Date
Mar 29, 2019
books recommendations

I sometimes get requests for book recommendations, so I decided to write a post on my favorite books.1 For each book, I give a very short description (< 3 sentences) of the book and why I enjoyed it. Hopefully, that’ll give you some idea of whether you want to read it too.

A quick plug: you might also be interested in following the books I read with my friend Kevin on our reading blog.

  1. Sapiens by Yuval Noah Harari

Harari tells a captivating story about how humans became the dominant species on the planet, with sweeping and intuitive explanations on a wide range of topics. Reading this book inspires you to think about society and human history from a broad perspective, in a way that is otherwise hard to do on your own.

  1. The New Jim Crow by Michelle Alexander

This was an incredibly eye-opening book for me. Alexander describes how the current justice system in America has (intentionally!) been set up to legitimize racial discrimination in present society; the fact that many remain unaware of its extent shows how horrifyingly successful the “new Jim Crow” has been. It’s a must-read for everyone living in America (and worth perusing for everyone else too).

  1. A Little Life by Hanya Yanagihara

I’ve never felt like I lived an entire life from reading a book until I read A Little Life. The characters are so heartachingly beautiful and the story is so immersive and emotional that when it all comes to an end, all you can say is wow, that was some story.

  1. When Breath Becomes Air by Paul Kalanithi

Kalanithi is a doctor who has to suddenly come to terms with his mortality when diagnosed with cancer. It’s a wonderful memoir written in a highly intelligent, thoughtful, and thought-provoking manner. I’ve set a reminder for myself to reread this book at a future date, so yes, I think it’s definitely worth reading at least once.

  1. The Righteous Mind by Jonathan Haidt

Jonathan writes a thought-provoking book about how we form our political opinions. He shows how differences in conservative and liberal thought can be explained by a moral basis, which by its mere formulation, goes a long way towards combating the instinctive demonization common in political discourse.

Honorable Mentions

The following books had perhaps a little bit less of an impact on me than the ones I listed above, but they’re all still tremendously enjoyable to read. I’ve provided a one-sentence synopsis for each book.

  • Behind the Beautiful Forevers by Katherine Boo - A story following residents in a slum in Mumbai and their very real struggles with poverty and corruption.
  • Being Mortal by Atul Gawande - A surgeon intersperses stories with criticisms and reflections on how medicine addresses end of life care.
  • Colorless Tsukuru Tazaki and His Years of Pilgrimage by Haruki Murakami - The protagonist sets out on a quest to find out what happened years ago that resulted in his four best friends cutting off all contact with him.
  • Half of a Yellow Sun by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie - A story set during the Nigerian Civil War, told through the different perspectives of several memorable characters.
  • The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August by Claire North - The protagonist is a time-traveler (sort of) that tries to stop the end of the world caused by another time-traveler.
  • The Pleasure of My Company by Steve Martin2 - A funny and fascinating character-driven story featuring a protagonist with OCD.
  • The Rosie Project by Graeme Simsion - Another funny story featuring a “socially-challenged” protagonist.
  • The White Tiger by Aravind Adiga - A boy in India leaves his village to become a taxi driver in an atypical rags-to-riches tale.

  1. Excluding childhood favorites.↩

  2. Did you know that Steve Martin (yes, the actor Steve Martin) wrote books? I had no idea.↩
