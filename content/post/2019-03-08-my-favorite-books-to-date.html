I sometimes get requests for book recommendations, so I decided to write a post on my favorite books.1 For each book, I give a very short description (< 3 sentences) of the book and why I enjoyed it. Hopefully, it’ll give you some idea of whether you’ll want to read it too.

You might also be interested in following the books I read with my friend Kevin on our reading blog, where we review every book we read (both the good and the bad).

  1. Sapiens by Yuval Noah Harari

Harari tells a captivating story about how humans became the dominant species on the planet, with sweeping and intuitive explanations on a wide range of topics. The book inspires you to think about society and human history from a broad perspective, which I think would’ve been hard to do on my own.

  1. The New Jim Crow by Michelle Alexander

This was an incredibly eye-opening book for me. Alexander describes how the current justice system in America has (intentionally!) been set up to legitimize racial discrimination in present society; that many remain unaware of its extent shows how horrifyingly successful the “new Jim Crow” has been. It’s a must-read for everyone in America (and worth perusing for everyone else too).

  1. A Little Life by Hanya Yanagihara

I’ve never felt like I lived an entire life from reading a book until I read A Little Life. It was some life too – the characters are so heartachingly beautiful and the story so emotional that when it all comes to an end, all you can say is wow.

  1. When Breath Becomes Air by Paul Kalanithi

Kalanithi is a doctor who has to suddenly come to terms with his mortality when diagnosed with cancer. It’s a wonderful memoir and could not have been written in a more intelligent, thoughtful, and thought-provoking manner. I’ve set a reminder for myself to reread this book at a future date, so yes, I think it’s definitely worth reading at least once.

  1. The Righteous Mind by Jonathan Haidt

Jonathan writes a thought-provoking book for anyone interested in politics. He describes a moral basis that gives rise to differences in conservative and liberal opinions, which by its mere formulation, goes a long way towards combating the instinctive demonization common in political discourse.

Honorable Mentions

The following books had perhaps a little bit less of an impact on me than the ones I listed above, but they’re all still tremendously enjoyable to read in their own way. I have provided a one-sentence synopsis for each book.

  • Behind the Beautiful Forevers by Katherine Boo - A story following residents in a slum in Mumbai and their very real struggles with poverty and corruption.
  • Being Mortal by Atul Gawande - A surgeon intersperses stories with criticisms and reflections on how medicine addresses end of life care.
  • Colorless Tsukuru Tazaki and His Years of Pilgrimage by Haruki Murakami - The protagonist sets out on a quest to find out what happened years ago that resulted in his four best friends cutting off all contact with him.
  • Half of a Yellow Sun by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie - A story set during the Nigerian Civil War, told through the different perspectives of several memorable characters.
  • The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August by Claire North - The protagonist is a time-traveler (sort of) that tries to stop the end of the world in a story that’s thrilling from start to finish.
  • The Pleasure of My Company by Steve Martin2 - A funny and fascinating character-driven story featuring a protagonist with OCD.
  • The Rosie Project by Graeme Simsion - Another funny and fascinating story featuring a “socially-challenged” protagonist.
  • The White Tiger by Aravind Adiga - The story follows a boy in India who leaves his village to become a taxi driver in an atypical rags-to-riches tale.

  1. Excluding childhood favorites.↩
